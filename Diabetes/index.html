LIVING WITH TYPE 2 DIABETES

OTA/PTA: Roya Sarwar

What is Type 2 Diabetes?

Type 2 diabetes is a chronic condition that affects the way the body metabolizes sugar (glucose) — an important source of fuel for the body. Type 2 diabetes develops when the body becomes resistant to insulin or when the pancreas is unable to produce enough insulin. When this occurs, glucose builds up in the blood instead of being used for energy.

The exact cause of this is unknown, although genetics and environmental factors, such as being overweight and inactive, seem to increase the risk of a type 2 diabetes diagnosis. The body gets sugar from foods like bread, potatoes, rice, pasta, milk and fruit. Your body needs insulin to use this sugar. Insulin is a hormone that helps your body control the level of sugar in your blood. The onset of type 2 diabetes can be gradual, and symptoms can be mild during the early stages. Many people may not realize that they have this condition. There's no cure for type 2 diabetes, but losing weight, eating healthy and exercising can help manage the disease. If diet and exercise aren't enough to manage your blood sugar, diabetes medications or insulin therapy may be needed. (Type 2 diabetes, 2019)

What is the role of insulin and glucose?

Insulin is a hormone that comes from the gland behind and below the pancreas.
  • The pancreas secretes insulin into the bloodstream.
  • The insulin circulates, enabling sugar to enter your cells.
  • Insulin lowers the amount of sugar in your bloodstream.
  • As your blood sugar level drops, so does the secretion of insulin from your pancreas. (Type 2 diabetes, 2019)
Glucose — a sugar — is a main source of energy for the cells that make up muscles and other tissues.
  • Glucose comes from two major sources: food and the liver.
  • Sugar is absorbed into the bloodstream, where it enters cells with the help of insulin.
  • Your liver stores and makes glucose.
  • When your glucose levels are low the liver breaks down stored glycogen into glucose to keep your glucose level within a normal range.

In type 2 diabetes, instead of moving into your cells, sugar builds up in the bloodstream. As blood sugar levels increase, the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas release more insulin, eventually these cells become impaired and can't make enough insulin to meet the body's demands. (Type 2 diabetes, 2019)

Signs and Symptoms

The symptoms of type 2 diabetes due to high blood sugar may include:
  • Blurry vision
  • Slow healing of cuts and wounds
  • Fatigue (weak, tired feeling)
  • Always feeling hungry
  • Increased thirst
  • Frequent urination
  • Headaches
  • Unexplained weight loss (even though you are eating and feel hungry)
  • Loss of consciousness (rare)

(Galan, 2018)

Prevention

You can decrease your risk of type 2 diabetes by:
  • Managing your weight, being overweight is a main risk factor for type 2 diabetes.
  • Fat distribution. If you store fat mainly in the abdomen, you have a greater risk of type 2 diabetes than if you store fat elsewhere, such as in your hips and thighs.
  • Increase your activity, inactivity increases the risk of type 2 diabetes.
  • Prediabetes. Prediabetes is a condition in which your blood sugar level is higher than normal, but not high enough to be classified as diabetes. Left untreated, prediabetes often progresses to type 2 diabetes.

(Galan, 2018)

Lifestyle Modifications

Medical management can include watching what you eat; monitoring your blood sugar, cholesterol, and blood pressure and checking in regularly with your doctor. (Diabetes management, 2017)

Nutrition: Carbohydrate counting is a flexible way to plan your meals. Felicite Wooley is a registered dietician and certified diabetes educator, watch her fascinating webinar from The Canadian Diabetes Association to learn more about how you can incorporate carb counting into your diabetes management plan and manage your blood glucose levels. https://youtu.be/R5k57QaVB-0
Exercise: When you exercise, your muscles use sugar (glucose) for energy. Regular physical activity also helps your body use insulin more efficiently. It is important to know what time of day is best to exercise so your workout routine is coordinated with your meal schedules. Talk to your doctor about what blood sugar levels are appropriate for you to begin exercise. Check your blood sugar level before, during and after exercise. It is important that you drink plenty of water while exercising because dehydration can affect blood sugar levels. Always has a snack with you during exercise and wear a medical identification bracelet while exercising.
Medication: Some people who have type 2 diabetes can achieve their target blood sugar levels with diet and exercise alone, but many also need diabetes medications or insulin therapy.
Smoking: The combination of high blood sugar and smoking increases damage to the blood vessels that feed the heart, brain, eyes, kidneys and peripheral nerves, speeding up the long-term complications of diabetes. It is important to take the first step and make the decision to quit. Get as much information as you can from your doctor or pharmacist about options to help you quit, including medications that can increase your chances of success. (Smoking and Diabetes, 2019) For more information, see Health Canada’s access free, convenient support for smokers. Trained specialists can provide counselling, help develop a quit smoking plan, answer questions and provide referrals to programs and services in your community. https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/smoking-tobacco/quit-smoking/tips-help-someone-quit-smoking/you-can-quit-smoking-we-can-help.html
Stress: Stress can be a major barrier to effective glucose control. Stress hormones in your body may directly affect glucose. You will learn relaxation techniques in your therapy sessions that can help relieve your stress and lower your blood sugar level. Progressive Muscle Relaxation is a wonderful relaxation technique that you can use at home, it helps induce a deep state of muscle relaxation. Physical relaxation leads to mental relaxation and mental calmness. PMR is known to be very effective in helping reduce anxiety, stress, and muscle tension. (Feldman, G., Greeson, J., & Senville, J. 2010) Relax and follow the instructions in this video whenever needed. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=86HUcX8ZtAk

Diabetes can be prevented, treated, controlled, and the risk lessened via various health and lifestyle interventions including physiotherapy. Physical activity is a crucial intervention to prevent and manage type 2 diabetes. Your therapy plan includes:
  • Warm up and cool down
  • Aerobic + strengthening exercises
  • Specific exercise to improve function and glycemic control
  • A focus on endurance and weight loss.
Therapy Plan: Diabetes Canada recommends getting 150 minutes of aerobic activity and three sessions of resistance activity each week.
